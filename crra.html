Today's lecture we used the the Constant Relative Risk Aversion (CRRA) utility function in order to understand the impact or interest rate on consumption. The standard CRRA function that you can see in text books etc looks like the one below. $$ u(c)= \begin{cases}\frac{1}{1-\theta} c^{1-\theta} & \text { if } \theta>0, \theta \neq 1 \\ \ln c & \text { if } \theta=1\end{cases} \tag{1}$$ In simple words, $\theta$ measures the degree of relative risk aversion which is implicit in the utility function. Although the function itself is the same we used a sligthly different version of this function. As you can see in our lecture note page 9 we used this $$U= {C^{1-\frac{1}{\sigma}}\over {1-\frac{1}{\sigma}}} \tag{2}$$ Our lifetime utility function is in other words our utility depends on two different consumption in two different period. $$ U=u\left(c_1\right)+\beta u\left(c_2\right) $$ Again for the simplicity let assume $\beta = 1$ and take the first derivative of the CRRA utility function (1) and we will end up this $$ u^{\prime}(c)=c^{-\theta}, $$ the marginal rate of substitution is $$ \frac{u^{\prime}\left(c_1\right)}{u^{\prime}\left(c_2\right)}=\frac{c_1^{-\theta}}{c_2^{-\theta}}=\left(\frac{c_2}{c_1}\right)^\theta $$ or, solving for $c_2 / c_1$ $$ \frac{c_2}{c_1}=\left(\frac{u^{\prime}\left(c_1\right)}{u^{\prime}\left(c_2\right)}\right)^{1 / \theta} $$ Thus, $$1/\theta $$ is the elasticity of the ratio of the consumed quantities of the two goods with respect to the marginal rate of substitution. **By definition $\sigma=1 / \theta$ is then the elasticity of substitution, which is constant for the CRRA utility function.** $$\sigma$$ is a measure of the strength of the substitution effect that a change in relative prices induces. In other words instead of using the equation-1 with $\theta$ we used equation-2 with $\sigma$ for $1 / \theta$. For that reason we wrote "$$\sigma$$ is the elasticity of inter-temporal substitution."
